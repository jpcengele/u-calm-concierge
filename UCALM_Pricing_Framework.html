What this framework is not.

Not a price list. These tiers never appear on the website. They never appear in marketing. They exist only as internal guidance for first conversations.

Not a sales tool. The framework is a discipline against price-following the market. It anchors what U-CALM should charge in operational capacity not in what competitors charge.

Not fixed. Each specific member relationship lands at a point within these ranges based on actual capacity demand, complexity, and introducer context.

The thesis in one sentence
U-CALM is priced by the file, not by the market. The capacity ceiling is not "how many members can U-CALM acquire" but "how many files can one specialist hold well." Industry observation suggests 6–20 active files per specialist before relationship quality breaks down. This framework prices that scarcity quietly.
Part I

The pricing landscape — four anchored benchmarks.

U-CALM does not need a precise read of every competitor's price card. It needs four anchored benchmarks at the edges of the market that frame the pricing conversation.

Benchmark 1 · Quintessentially Elite — the floor for credible UHNW concierge.

The floor for any U-CALM tier is therefore in the £25,000+ range, even at the lightest entry profile. Below this floor, the brand register starts to be confused with mid-tier concierge.

Part II

The capacity principle.

U-CALM is not priced by what competitors charge. It is priced by what one specialist can hold well. The capacity ceiling is the unit of pricing, not market rate.

Why capacity is the correct anchor.

A mass-platform concierge can absorb additional members at near-zero marginal cost — the next member is a tiny dilution of an already-pooled specialist team. The marginal economics of an extra member at Ten Lifestyle Group are very different from the marginal economics of an extra member at U-CALM, where each member adds load to a named, multi-decade-relationship specialist.

The natural pricing question for a platform is: "what will the market pay?"

The natural pricing question for U-CALM is: "what is each remaining named-specialist slot worth?"

These are different questions. The first invites price competition. The second invites scarcity.

The 6/12/20 rule.

The recommended internal capacity guidelines, by tier:

  • Office tier — 6 files per specialist. Personal-office intensity. Aviation, protection, household, multiple residences, multi-generation family file. This is the Knightsbridge Circle Elite cap (4:1 + some slack).
  • House tier — 12 files per specialist. Standard multi-residence relationship; aviation regular; protection on file but not constant. Industry middle ground.
  • Foundation tier — 20 files per specialist. Single-residence concierge with occasional aviation; lighter coordination load.

These caps are the most important operational discipline in this framework. They are also the most important pricing input. When a specialist's roster approaches the cap, the next prospective member's price moves up the range, not down. If the cap is reached, the next member waits for capacity or is referred away. This is not a sales policy. It is a brand policy.

The Hutchinson failure mode, internalised. Amelia Hutchinson described herself as "run down, tired, living on phone" — the documented end-state of one-person UHNW concierge without capacity discipline. The 6/12/20 rule is the operational defence against that mode. Pricing must support it; pricing must never undermine it.

How capacity translates to pricing.

If a House-tier specialist holds 12 files at an average CHF 90,000, their portfolio generates CHF 1.08m of annual revenue. After operator pass-through, infrastructure, support staff and partner-network costs, this is the unit economic that funds the brand. If price falls below capacity, the unit economics break — and so does the brand promise. Holding price is therefore not greed; it is the only way the model works.

Notes on the tier structure.

The House tier is the centre of gravity. Most U-CALM members will sit in the House tier, with their actual annual figure landing in the £65–120k range depending on residence count, aviation cadence, and family complexity. This is the natural "default" U-CALM relationship.

The Foundation tier exists for early-career UHNW members. A successful tech founder in their late thirties, recently liquid, with one Milan residence and growing aviation needs, may not yet need House. Foundation lets the relationship begin small. Over years, members tend to migrate up — Foundation members become House members as their lives become more complex; House members become Office members at family-office moments.

The Office tier should remain genuinely scarce. At most 12 Office-tier files at any time across the entire U-CALM house. This protects the personal-office intensity and the capacity discipline. Office tier members are a category of relationship, not a category of revenue maximisation.

Aviation operator costs are itemised separately on every statement, regardless of tier. The membership figures above are coordination fees only. A member who flies 25 hours per year at NetJets-equivalent rates is paying an additional €212k+ in pass-through operator costs. The membership figure is for the named specialist and the integrated file, not for the aircraft itself.

The architecture in one frame
Foundation builds the relationship. House is where members live. Office is where they retire to. The tiers are not a price ladder; they are a model of how a UHNW relationship matures.
Part IV

U-Calm Aviation — pricing the integration premium.

U-Calm Aviation does not own aircraft. It does not sell hours. It sells the integration of flight with everything else. The pricing question is therefore: what is integration worth?

What U-Calm Aviation is not.

It is not NetJets Europe. NetJets sells hours on its own fleet at €8,480/hour with a 25-hour annual minimum (€212,000). NetJets owns the aircraft, employs the crew, controls the operational base, and bears the fleet capital cost. The price reflects all of that.

It is not VistaJet. VistaJet's VJ25 card on a Challenger 350 sits at approximately $15,000/hour, three-year minimum commitment, $375,000+ first-year cost. Same ownership-and-fleet model as NetJets, different airframe.

It is not a charter broker. A broker takes a commission on each flight and disappears between flights. The relationship is transactional and the price is loaded into the per-flight margin.

U-Calm Aviation is the named specialist who knows your week and arranges the aircraft for you. The aircraft itself is sourced from a vetted operator panel; the operator does the flying; U-Calm Aviation does the coordination. The pricing question is what that coordination is worth, separately and transparently.

The four-hour notice window as a member benefit.

Enrolled members in the House and Office tiers have access to a four-hour notice window on eligible aircraft. This is not a discount; it is an availability guarantee, achievable only because U-Calm Aviation holds a vetted operator panel with named availability commitments. The cost of holding this availability is folded into the membership fee, not charged per-event.

This is the most distinctive single feature of U-Calm Aviation versus charter brokers. Charter brokers cannot offer it because they don't pay to hold availability; they only quote when asked.

The conversation framing for aviation pricing. "You are already paying €212,000 a year to NetJets for hours in the air on a single aircraft type. Our membership covers the same operational standards across a wider panel, integrated with the rest of your day, with a four-hour notice window — and the operator costs of each flight are pass-through, itemised, no mark-up. You pay us for the file. You pay the operator for the aircraft. One statement."
Part V

How to introduce price in a first conversation.

The pricing conversation is the single hardest moment in any UHNW relationship. Done well, it confirms the member's instinct that they are in the right room. Done poorly, it converts the conversation into a transaction.

The five-step conversation pattern.

One · Establish the file before establishing the price. In a first conversation — typically over a quiet hour somewhere considered — the U-CALM principal asks about the member's life. Where do they live across the year. Who travels with them. What patterns have grown around the family. This is not discovery in the sales sense; it is the beginning of the file. Price does not come up.

Two · The introducer's frame is the price frame. When the conversation reaches price, the principal references how the introducer described U-CALM — the family office partner, the lawyer, the private banker. "As [introducer] mentioned, U-CALM is structured around a small number of long-term relationships. The membership reflects that." The introducer's authority does the heavy lifting; U-CALM does not have to defend the number.

Three · State the range, anchored against capacity not market. "Membership for a relationship of this shape sits between CHF [lower] and CHF [upper] per year. The number depends on the complexity of the file we hold and the cadence of work — not on a published rate." The capacity language is decisive. The member hears: we are not selling subscription; we are committing capacity.

Four · Place aviation pass-through context immediately after. The membership figure can sound substantial in isolation. Anchor it against what the member already pays for one service line alone. "For context, a member who flies twenty-five hours a year on a private charter card pays approximately €212,000 to the operator alone. Our membership coordinates everything against that — the flight, the ground, the household, the school — and that figure is itemised separately from the membership."

Five · Close with the file, not the fee. The conversation does not end on price. It ends on what the file holds — a return to the texture of the relationship the member would have. "What matters is that the relationship is held by one person who knows your year. The membership is what allows us to hold that, well, for a small enough number of families that no one is asked to wait."

What to avoid in the first pricing conversation.

  • Do not produce a written price card. Even a discreet leave-piece with prices on it converts the relationship to a brochure interaction.
  • Do not compare U-CALM to Quintessentially. Members will draw the comparison themselves if they wish; U-CALM introducing it diminishes the brand.
  • Do not negotiate on the first conversation. The range is the range. If a member tries to negotiate, that is information about whether the relationship is the right fit — not a price discussion.
  • Do not offer discounts for multi-year commitment. Multi-year is the default expectation, not an upgrade. No member who needs a discount is the right member.
  • Do not name the tiers. "Foundation, House, Office" are internal categories. Members do not need to know which tier they are in. They are in U-CALM.
Part VI

Member-value framing — six articulations.

Six ways to articulate what the member is buying. Use depending on the prospect's frame. None of them lead with the word "concierge."

Frame 1 · The single statement.

"Aviation, ground, household, protection — held by one person on one file, billed on one statement at month-end. The membership is what makes that possible."

Best for: members who currently use three or four separate suppliers and feel the fragmentation. Bankers, lawyers, multi-residence families.

Frame 2 · The named person.

"You have a named specialist who knows your family. Not a team. Not a queue. The same person, for as long as the relationship runs. The membership is what allows us to hold the file at that intensity for a small enough number of families that no one waits."

Best for: members who have been disappointed by Quintessentially or a card-bundled concierge. Direct contrast with platform models.

Frame 3 · The capacity-not-rate framing.

"We don't price against market rates. We price against capacity — how many files one specialist can hold well. That is what determines whether the relationship works."

Best for: financially sophisticated members who recognise the difference between a subscription and a commitment. Family-office principals, bankers, founders.

Frame 4 · The contrast with what they already pay.

"A NetJets Europe entry jet card is €212,000 a year. That's twenty-five hours in the air on one aircraft type, no ground integration, no household. Our membership coordinates everything against that — and the flight cost is itemised separately, pass-through, no mark-up."

Best for: aviation-heavy members already paying six figures for one service line. The frame makes the U-CALM number look modest.

Frame 5 · The personal-office framing.

"For families who have considered a personal office — a head of household staff, a chief-of-staff role — U-CALM is the externalised version. One specialist, one file, integration across services. Without the cost and complexity of in-house employment."

Best for: members thinking about scale of household management as their family grows. Compares well to in-house salaries (£150k–£250k for a head-of-household role plus benefits).

Frame 6 · The introducer-quoted framing.

"As [introducer] said when they suggested we meet, U-CALM operates by a small number of long-term relationships. That structure determines everything — including how the membership is priced."

Best for: any conversation in which the introducer is held in high trust. The introducer's authority is the conversation's authority. Use sparingly to preserve the trust.

The six frames, in shorthand
Statement · Person · Capacity · Contrast · Office · Introducer. Each works for a different prospect at a different moment. The principle is constant: lead with what the relationship holds; arrive at the number only after the texture of the relationship is clear in the member's mind.
Part VII

Annual review and revision process.

This framework should be reviewed once a year — no more, no less. Pricing stability is itself a brand signal.

What to review annually.

  1. The four anchor benchmarks. Have Quintessentially Elite, Knightsbridge Circle entry, KsC Elite, or NetJets Europe Card25 changed price? If yes, by how much? Knightsbridge Circle Elite is the most important to watch — any movement in the £100k figure signals industry-wide repricing.
  2. The capacity ratios. Are the 6/12/20 file caps holding? If any specialist's roster is over-cap, are they showing strain? If under-cap, is the membership growing as expected?
  3. The pass-through cost trajectory. Operator hourly rates, FBO fees, fixer day rates. Inflation, fuel, supply-demand. Membership figures should be reviewed if pass-through structurally rises >15% in a year.
  4. The introducer feedback. What did introducers hear in price conversations? Where did prospective members balk? Where did they accept without hesitation? The introducer voice is the most important pricing signal U-CALM receives.
  5. The competitive movement. Knightsbridge Circle continental expansion. New invitation-tier entrants in Milan or Lugano. Quintessentially Quintessence pricing changes.

What to revise vs. hold steady.

Revise: the upper bound of each tier range, if the four anchors have moved meaningfully. Pass-through itemisation conventions on statements. Conversation framing language as the market matures.

Hold steady: the three-tier structure (Foundation / House / Office) — this is a brand architecture, not a pricing decision. The 6/12/20 capacity rule. The "no published price card" principle. The introducer-mediated entry pattern.

Never: publish the tiers, the prices, or the structure outside of an active member conversation. The internal-only discipline is more important than any individual price within the structure.

One paragraph to remember.

U-CALM is priced by the file, not by the market. Each named-specialist relationship is a finite resource. The 6/12/20 capacity rule is the operating discipline; the membership tier is its pricing expression; the conversation framing makes both legible to the member. Hold the discipline, and the pricing holds itself.
